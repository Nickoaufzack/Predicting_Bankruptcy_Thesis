\chapter{Feature Importance}
\label{ch:featureimportance}

Having established how the models are trained and evaluated, this chapter turns to
the interpretive question of the thesis, which financial features the fitted models
actually rely on. Three importance methods are applied to each of the three models:
native importance, permutation importance and SHAP. They are introduced here as
methods, while their numerical results are reported in Chapter~\ref{ch:results}.
These rankings describe what the models rely on to separate bankrupt from healthy
firms, not the mechanisms that cause insolvency and are therefore to be read as
associative rather than causal.

The second problem raised in Chapter~\ref{ch:dataset} returns here, because it
determines how the following rankings can be read. The redundancy among the $95$
ratios is structural, as all of them are derived from the same balance sheet and
income statement. \emph{Debt ratio \%} and \emph{Net worth/Assets} sum to exactly one
for every firm in the sample and are therefore the same quantity written twice,
while ROA(A), ROA(B) and ROA(C) differ only in the treatment of interest and
depreciation and correlate between $0.94$ and $0.99$. Across 
the whole feature set the correlation is nevertheless low, with a mean
absolute Pearson coefficient of $0.08$ and a median of $0.02$, which is consistent
with \textcite[5]{wangliu2021} and \textcite[7]{ansahnarh2024} reporting no substantial
multicollinearity on this data. Both observations hold at once because correlation
is measured pairwise. Of the $93$ continuous features, $31$ have at least one partner
above $0.9$, but each of them is redundant with a median of two partners out of $92$
and unrelated to the rest. These $28$ pairs are $0.65\%$ of all $4{,}278$ pairs, so
their near-perfect values disappear into an average dominated by the remaining ones.
A third of the features has a twin, and the global statistic still shows almost
nothing.

These features are kept rather than screened out, following \textcite{wangliu2021} in
preserving the integrity of the data. The redundancy therefore reaches the
interpretation stage intact and the three methods that follow each handle it
differently. That is what makes the comparison informative and it is why the
rankings in Chapter~\ref{ch:results} have to be read against one another rather than
in isolation.

\section{Native Importance}
\label{sec:fi-native}

Native importance reads the ranking directly off the fitted model and therefore
differs by model family. For the logistic regression it is the vector of coefficients from
Equation~\eqref{eq:lasso}. They carry a sign, and since the $\ell_1$ penalty has
already dropped the redundant twin from each correlated group, each surviving
coefficient stands on its own. This is the one model that reaches the interpretation stage with
the redundancy already resolved.

For the two tree ensembles it is the Gini impurity importance of
Section~\ref{sec:rf}, which is unsigned and carries two distortions. 
\textcite{strobl2007} show that impurity importance leans towards features that take
many different values, simply because they offer more points at which to split. With
$93$ continuous ratios, that bias applies here directly. 
Redundancy adds a second one. When two ratios are near-identical, a split can use
only one of them at a time, meaning across the forest the credit is shared between the
twins. The importance of what they jointly carry is split in two and each ends up
looking less important than the pair really is. Neither distortion shows up in the
ranking on its own, which is why the Gini importance is not read in isolation.

\section{Permutation Importance}
\label{sec:fi-permutation}

Permutation importance is model-agnostic and based on performance. After the models
are fitted, it asks how much the performance drops if we ``disregard'' one feature.
For one feature at a time, its values are shuffled across the firms, so that the
feature keeps its overall distribution but no longer lines up with which firms went
bankrupt. Everything else is left untouched and how far the $F_2$-score then drops
is recorded.

A feature the model genuinely relies on causes a large drop when shuffled, while a
feature it hardly uses would not move the score as much. Averaging this over ten repetitions then
makes sure this does not come down to a single lucky or unlucky shuffle,
following the same logic as cross-validation. And because the measure is defined
purely through the loss of the same $F_2$ the models were selected on, it applies
agnostically to all three and gives a common anchor for comparing them.

Its weakness is the redundancy from before. When a feature has a near-identical
twin, shuffling one of them changes little, because the twin still holds most of the
same information. Even a feature the model relies on can then look
unimportant. This is the counterpart to the impurity case where redundancy splits
the credit between twins, here it hides it. It is not a flaw in the method but 
rather a property of permutation under dependence and the reason why the three 
methods are compared against eachother in
Chapter~\ref{ch:results}.

\section{SHAP}
\label{sec:fi-shap}

SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) bases feature importance on game theory and
differs from the previous two methods in the sense that it comes with axiomatic
guarantees \parencite{lundberg2017}. The idea is to rebuild a single prediction from
simple, additive pieces with a starting value plus one contribution for each feature.
\textcite{lundberg2017} write this as
\begin{equation}
  g(z') = \phi_0 + \sum_{i=1}^{95} \phi_i z_i',
  \label{eq:shap-additive}
\end{equation}
Here $g(z')$ is an explanation model, a replica that rebuilds the model's true
prediction by adding different pieces together. The first is the base value $\phi_0$,
an average prediction across all firms, before the model knows anything about the
specific firm. The others are contributions $\phi_i$ of the individual features,
added on top of that base. Each $\phi_i$ is the so-called Shapley value of feature $i$, through which we can see
just how important each feature is by its actual numeric contribution to the model's
prediction. Under the axioms behind SHAP these pieces return the true
prediction of the model exactly, which is the additive guarantee the method is named
for. The switch $z_i' \in \{0,1\}$ only states whether
feature $i$ is included ($1$) or left out ($0$).

The question is then how to find each contribution $\phi_i$, the Shapley value of
feature $i$. It is the one fair way, taken from cooperative game theory, to split the
``payout'' of a prediction among the features, treating them as players that
cooperate. A feature's contribution is how much it changes the prediction when it
joins a group of the other features, or coalition, averaged over every possible
group. Writing $F$ for the full set of features, the model with feature $i$ present,
$f_{S \cup \{i\}}$, is compared with the model without it, $f_S$, across every group
$S$ of the remaining features:
\begin{equation}
  \phi_i = \sum_{S \subseteq F \setminus \{i\}}
  \frac{|S|!\,\bigl(|F| - |S| - 1\bigr)!}{|F|!}
  \left[\, f_{S \cup \{i\}}\!\left(x_{S \cup \{i\}}\right) - f_S\!\left(x_S\right) \right].
  \label{eq:shap-shapley}
\end{equation}
The fraction in front weights each group by how many orders feature $i$ could have
joined it in, so that $\phi_i$ is a weighted average of what the feature adds across
all groups. That averaging is what makes the split fair, but it is also what makes it
impossible to compute directly. There is one group for every combination of the
other features, $2^{|F|-1}$ of them, so with $95$ features this is $2^{94}$ groups per
feature, far beyond anything that can be listed out.

The way around this is to use the structure of each model to reach the same values
without ever listing the groups. For the logistic regression, a linear explainer
(LinearSHAP) reads the Shapley values directly from the coefficients. For the two
tree ensembles, a tree explainer (TreeSHAP) walks through the trees and recovers the
exact values in a tiny fraction of the time. In both cases the values are computed
for the bankruptcy class across all firms, and a feature's overall importance is
summarised by the mean absolute SHAP value across firms, reported in
Chapter~\ref{ch:results}.

Two properties make SHAP the interpretive centre of gravity for this thesis. First,
it returns \emph{signed} attributions for \emph{all three} models: the logistic
coefficients are signed but only linear, and the impurity importance is unsigned,
whereas SHAP places every model on the same signed, additive axis and so makes their
rankings directly comparable. Second, because it looks at whole groups of features
rather than changing one feature at a time, it does not collapse under the
correlation that so weakens permutation importance. Where the three methods disagree,
as they will in Chapter~\ref{ch:results}, SHAP provides the most trustworthy reading,
while permutation importance keeps its value precisely because its collapse diagnoses
the redundancy of the data.
