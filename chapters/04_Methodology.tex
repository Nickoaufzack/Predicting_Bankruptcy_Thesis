\chapter{Methodology}
\label{ch:methodology}

This chapter builds the methodological backbone of the thesis, while tackling the first forementioned problem of class imbalance. 
It evolves around the pipeline and tools used, which are grounded in the relevant scientific 
theory to build the prediction model. While others handle the correlation through feature selection already at this stage 
(\textcite{pham2025}, \textcite{chaudhary2023}), this thesis takes a different approach by working with all the data to 
"preserve the integrity" (\textcite[5]{wangliu2021}) and address the redundancy from high correlation in the contribution of 
this thesis in Chapter~\ref{ch:featureimportance}. The guiding idea is a pipeline that is reproducible at every step, follows 
established best practices, and trains only on real, historically observed firms rather than synthetic samples. 
The pipeline rests on six core components: data preprocessing, undersampling and the prediction models, which are all 
wrapped inside a cross-validation loop that, through an embedded hyperparameter search, gives us different performance metrics.


\section{Data Preprocessing}
\label{sec:preprocessing}

Before we can apply any machine learning algorithms, the features of the data
must first be unified, or else specific features, just by the nature of their
units, would dominate the learning process. The transformation applied to the
features is a min--max normalisation, which rescales each feature onto the
interval $[0,1]$, making them comparable. Writing $F$ for the set of
values a given feature takes across all firms, every value $x \in F$ is mapped to
\begin{equation}
  x' \;=\; \frac{x - \min(F)}{\max(F) - \min(F)}, \qquad \forall\, x \in F,
  \label{eq:minmax}
\end{equation}
where $\min(F)$ and $\max(F)$ are the smallest and largest values of that
feature. We follow this normalisation used by the dataset's originators
\parencite[562--563]{liang2016} and by \textcite[5]{wangliu2021}, and implement
it with scikit-learn's \texttt{MinMaxScaler}. % Two features, the
% \emph{Liability-Assets Flag} and the \emph{Net Income Flag}, are already binary
% $0/1$ indicators rather than continuous ratios and are passed through unchanged.

% The choice of scaling follows from the specific Pipeline used rather than from the
% data alone. Our two tree based classifiers were going to cover in 
% chapter~\ref{sec:Random Forrest} and chapter~\ref{sec:XGBoost} for example are invariant 
% to monotone transformations of the features. Two other components, however,
% are scale-sensitive. Logistic regression is not scale-invariant --- under an
% $\ell_1$ or $\ell_2$ penalty the scale of a feature affects how strongly its
% coefficient is shrunk --- so a common scale is needed for the penalty to treat
% all coefficients comparably; and the \emph{ClusterCentroids} undersampler
% (Section~\ref{sec:undersampling}) relies on Euclidean distances, which are only
% meaningful once features share a scale. Min--max normalisation supplies that
% scale. This is where we depart from \textcite{wangliu2021}, who additionally
% apply a logarithmic transformation to reduce skewness: that step suits their
% distribution-sensitive naive-Bayes classifier, whereas our rank-based ensembles
% and penalised linear model gain nothing from it, so we keep preprocessing to the
% single min--max step. The scaler's minimum and maximum are estimated on each
% fold's training portion only, never on the full dataset, so that no test
% information leaks into training; the fold mechanics follow in
% Section~\ref{sec:cv}.


\section{Undersampling}
\label{sec:undersampling}

After the data has been scaled, the first step of the pipeline is to restore class
balance so that a model can actually learn to recognise the minority. Two routes
exist: undersampling \emph{removes} observations from the majority (healthy) class, while
oversampling \emph{adds} new observations to the minority (bankrupt) class.

\textcite{batista2004} gives a thorough overview of the available methods. For
undersampling, the most prominent are Tomek Links, Edited Nearest Neighbours 
or the Neighbourhood Cleaning Rule. However, these methods mostly remove noise or 
target local outliers in the majority class and on
their own do little to correct a severe imbalance \parencite[23]{batista2004}. Therefore, 
part of the literature also turns to oversampling methods such as SMOTE (Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique), 
which generate synthetic minority observations by interpolating between existing ones \parencite{chawla2002}. 
This is not to say that such methods are not well established or scientifically justified, 
their validity is not the dispute of this thesis. The problem is rather that they are often 
applied in a way that improves reported performance.
When oversampling precedes evaluation,
models are scored partly on firms that never existed. The guiding idea of this
thesis is instead a pipeline that uses only real, historically observed firms and
is built around a reproducible framework rather than the pursuit of maximal
performance. %TODO: check the statement, do we need proof here? 
For this reason, this thesis undersamples exclusively and follows
the framework of \textcite{wangliu2021}, using \emph{ClusterCentroids} to do so.


\emph{ClusterCentroids} builds on $K$-means clustering \parencite[Ch.~12.4.1]{islp2023}.
The idea is to shrink the majority class to $K$ most representative firms and train
the model on those. Following Algorithm~12.2 of \textcite[523]{islp2023}, each firm
is first assigned at random to one of $K$ clusters, which serves as the starting
point. The algorithm then repeats two steps until the assignments no longer change:
(a) for each cluster it computes the centroid, the mean of all firms currently
assigned to it, and (b) it reassigns each firm to the cluster whose centroid is
closest in Euclidean distance. Figure~\ref{fig:kmeans} illustrates this procedure.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{kmeans_islp.png}
  \caption{$K$-means clustering on illustrative synthetic
    two-dimensional data with $K=3$. Step 1: randomly assign each observation to a cluster; 
    Step 2:(a) compute the centroid for each cluster; (b) reassign each observation to the closest cluster.
    The actual procedure condenses $5{,}939$ healthy firms into $660$
    representatives inside the Cross-validation pipepline.}
  \label{fig:kmeans}
  \floatfoot{Source: \textcite{islp2023}.}
\end{figure}

The resulting centroids are synthetic averages of several firms, so using them
directly to train would reintroduce synthetic data this thesis avoids. Instead,
for each cluster we keep a \emph{real} firm that is nearest to its centroid, i.e.\ its most
representative member, and train on those. In \texttt{imbalanced-learn} this
behaviour is the \texttt{voting=\textquotesingle hard\textquotesingle} setting. The
result is a downsized majority class that stays as representative of the original
distribution as possible, using only genuine firms. The degree of balancing is set
by the \texttt{sampling\_strategy=0.3}, meaning the bankrupt
firms will make up $30\%$ of the now undersampled majority. This value is fixed rather
than tuned, chosen for comparability with \textcite{wangliu2021}. The full pipeline
with the per-fold counts will be covered in Section~\ref{sec:cv}.



\section{Prediction Models}
\label{sec:models}

After the data has been scaled and undersampled, three classifiers are trained and
compared: logistic regression, random forest and gradient boosting. The aim is not
to isolate a single best model but to see what each added layer of complexity
contributes. Every model earns its place and, as the feature-importance analysis
will show, its own view on which features matter. What is meant by complexity 
is captured by the bias--variance trade-off
\parencite[Sec.~2.2.2]{islp2023}. Bias is the error a model makes because its
assumptions are too "simple" to capture the true relationship, variance describes 
how much its predictions change when it is trained on a different sample. 
Adding complexity lowers bias but raises variance. Logistic
regression sits at the beginning: a linear, low-variance baseline whose simplicity
is also its main limitation. The two tree ensembles are more flexible and reduce
the error from opposite sides. The Random Forest mainly by trying to lower variance,
gradient boosting mainly by lowering bias. This is why all three are included
alongside one another.


\subsection{Logistic Regression}
\label{sec:lr}

Logistic regression is the linear baseline of the comparison. It takes a linear 
combination of the $95$ financial ratios and passes it
through the sigmoid function, which maps the result into the interval
$(0,1)$. The coefficients are estimated by maximum likelihood 
and the resulting scale is in log-odds rather then probabilities
\parencite[Sec.~4.3]{islp2023}.

For this thesis the more relevant part is regularisation, because it
is what adapts the model to a dataset of $95$ partly redundant ratios. Left
unregularised, correlated features let the coefficients grow large and unstable,
and every ratio keeps a non-zero weight, which harms both stability and
readability. Regularisation counters this by adding a penalty on the size of the
coefficients to the fitting objective. Through our pipeline the $\ell_1$
(Lasso) penalty \parencite{tibshirani1996} is selected, which estimates the 
coefficients as
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}
  = \arg\min_{\boldsymbol{\beta}}
    \Bigl\{ -\ell(\boldsymbol{\beta}) + \lambda \sum_{j=1}^{95} |\beta_j| \Bigr\},
  \label{eq:lasso}
\end{equation}

where $\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}$ is the set of coefficients that makes this
expression smallest, $\ell(\boldsymbol{\beta})$ is the log-likelihood, which enters with
a minus sign because minimising its negative is the same as maximising it and
$\lambda$ sets the strength of the penalty placed on the sum over the features,
which \texttt{scikit-learn} parametrises through $C = 1/\lambda$. What
differentiates the LASSO from Ridge is that we take the absolute value of the
coefficients rather than the squared value, which gives the model the ability to
drive some coefficients exactly to zero and therefore perform an implicit feature
selection: from a group of strongly correlated ratios it tends to keep one
representative and discard the rest, which both tames the multicollinearity
discussed in Chapter~\ref{ch:dataset} and yields a sparser, more readable model.

This makes logistic regression the interpretable anchor of the comparison. Every
surviving coefficient carries a sign, so the model states not only which ratios
matter but in which direction they push a firm towards or away from bankruptcy. 
The price for this transparency is rigidity. Being linear, it cannot represent the threshold effects and
interactions between ratios that the tree ensembles exploit, which is why it takes
the role of the low-variance, higher-bias baseline of the comparison. That
same signed, transparent structure is what will later make the three importance
methods in Chapter~\ref{ch:featureimportance} agree most cleanly for this model.


\subsection{Random Forest}
\label{sec:rf}

The random forest removes the linear restriction of the previous model by building
on decision trees. A tree splits the data step by step at thresholds on single
ratios like ``Debt ratio $> x$'' for example and can in this way capture the
interactions between ratios that a linear model cannot. A
single decision tree is low in bias but high in variance, meaning it learns a
particular training sample very closely and is therefore unstable. This is why we utilise
the random forest \parencite{breiman2001}. It grows many trees, each on its own bootstrap
sample drawn with replacement from the training firms and lets them vote.
Averaging over many trees cancels much of the variance of any individual tree. In addition,
what gives the classifier its name, at each split, only a random subset of the features is
considered, which decorrelates the trees so that the averaging actually pays off. On $95$ partly
redundant ratios this decorrelation is what keeps the forest from building a
set of near-identical trees and the size of that subset (\texttt{max\_features})
is correspondingly one of its main tuning parameters \parencite{probst2019}.

What holds the construction together is the rule by which each split is chosen. At
every node the tree tries out possible thresholds and keeps the one that leaves the
two resulting groups as pure as it can, meaning as close as possible to containing
only healthy or only bankrupt firms. How mixed a group still is gets measured by the
Gini index, which for a node $t$ with class proportions $p_k$ is
\begin{equation}
  G(t) = \sum_{k} p_k\,(1 - p_k) ,
  \label{eq:gini}
\end{equation}
which is zero when a node contains a single class and largest at 0.5 when the classes are
evenly mixed \parencite[Sec.~8.1]{islp2023}. Because every split is selected to
lower this quantity, a feature that cleanly separates bankrupt from healthy firms
yields large impurity reductions. Summed over all splits and trees this is also what
the forest reports as its native feature importance. Unlike the signed coefficients
of the logistic regression it only records that a ratio matters, not in which
direction. That leaves the interpretation open and is one reason
for the comparison in Chapter~\ref{ch:featureimportance}.

In bias--variance terms this places the random forest on the other side of the
logistic regression. Where the linear baseline was stable but seemingly too simple to capture
the true relationship, the deep trees of the forest are flexible enough to have low
bias and the bootstrapping and decorrelation strip away much of the instability. 
The forest therefore lowers variance while keeping bias low.

\subsection{Gradient Boosting}
\label{subsec:gb}

Gradient boosting uses the same building block of decision trees as the random forest,
but combines them in the opposite way. Where the random forest grows many
deep trees independently and averages them to cut variance, gradient boosting grows
flat trees sequentially, each one correcting the errors of its predecessors 
and in this way targets bias \parencite{friedman2001}. The two therefore
attack the error from opposite sides, which is precisely why both are included
alongside the linear baseline.

The model is built up in stages. It starts from a simple initial prediction $F_0$
and improves it one tree at a time. At each stage $m$ it looks at the residuals of
the current ensemble, the part of the outcome it still gets wrong, fits a new small
tree $h_m$ to exactly these residuals and adds it to the model,
\begin{equation}
  F_m(x) = F_{m-1}(x) + \nu\, h_m(x), \qquad
  h_m \text{ fitted to } r = y - F_{m-1}(x),
  \label{eq:gb}
\end{equation}
where $F_{m-1}$ is the ensemble after the previous stage and $\nu$ is the learning
rate \parencite[Eq.~36, p.~1203]{friedman2001}. A new tree therefore never starts
over. It only learns where the ensemble still misjudges firms, for instance
bankrupt ones it currently scores too low and $\nu$ decides how much of that
correction is actually applied.

The name gradient boosting comes from these residuals. In mathematical terms the
expression $y - F_{m-1}(x)$ is the negative gradient of the squared-error loss, and
fitting each tree to it is what gives the method its name. The classification loss
used here follows the same principle.

A single flat tree has high bias and low variance and the sequence of corrections
lowers that bias step by step. The risk is that the process keeps correcting until
it starts fitting noise, which is where overfitting would set in. Two settings of the
\texttt{scikit-learn} implementation used in the pipeline hold it back: each tree is
kept flat through a small \texttt{max\_depth}, which here means something different
than in the random forest, where the trees are instead grown deep and the
contribution of every tree is scaled down by the \texttt{learning\_rate} $\nu$, so
that bias is reduced gradually over many small steps rather than a few aggressive
ones.

\section{Cross-Validation}
\label{sec:cv}

Now coming to the pipeline, which ties everything up to this point together through
cross-validation. The specific form used here is a repeated stratified $k$-fold
cross-validation with a nested grid search.
Rather than relying on a single train/test split, whose result would depend heavily 
on which firms happen to land in the test set, cross-validation rotates 
the test set so that every firm is predicted exactly once. 
In its $k$-fold form the data are divided into
$k = 10$ equally sized folds, each of which serves once as the test set while the
remaining nine form the training set. Because bankruptcies make up only $3.23\%$ of
the sample, the folds are also drawn in a stratified way, so that each one reproduces
this global class ratio, without this a fold could end up with almost no
bankrupt firms and make the estimate meaningless.

Figure~\ref{fig:cv} shows how this looks on our data. The starting point is the full
dataset of $6{,}819$ firms, $6{,}599$ healthy and $220$ bankrupt, which is divided
into the ten folds. In each of them the training set contains $5{,}939$ healthy and
$198$ bankrupt firms, and the held-out test fold contains the remaining $660$ healthy
and $22$ bankrupt firms, that is $682$ firms in total. Nine folds train while one
fold tests and the test set moves across the folds until every firm has been
evaluated exactly once.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{cv_folds.png}
  \caption{Repeated stratified ten-fold cross-validation. In each fold nine parts
    train and one part (orange) is held out for testing; the test fold rotates
    through all ten folds so that every firm is predicted once. Stratification keeps
    the $3.23\%$ bankruptcy rate in every fold, splitting the $6{,}599$ healthy and
    $220$ bankrupt firms in the same proportion.}
  \label{fig:cv}
  \floatfoot{Source: own.}
\end{figure}

What happens inside a single training fold is the part that must be protected
against information leakage. The preprocessing of Section~\ref{sec:preprocessing}
and the undersampling of Section~\ref{sec:undersampling} are not applied to the
whole dataset in advance, but are re-fitted within each training fold, chained
together with the classifier in an \texttt{imbalanced-learn} pipeline. When the
pipeline is fitted on a fold it runs in order. The \texttt{MinMaxScaler} first
learns its minima and maxima from the $5{,}939 + 198$ training rows. The
\texttt{ClusterCentroids} sampler then applies the sampling strategy of $30\%$ and
condenses the $5{,}939$ healthy majority class to $198 / 0.3 = 660$. All $198$
bankrupt firms are kept, which results in a training set of $858$ firms. Only these
rows are passed to the model. Because the scaler and the sampler never see the test
fold, no information from the held-out firms can leak into the fitted model.

Prediction on the test fold uses the same pipeline, only with the undersampler
skipped. The $682$ test firms therefore keep their real, untouched
$96.8/3.2$ distribution. Training on the balanced $858$-firm set while evaluating on
this full, imbalanced data is what makes the reported scores a robust and honest
estimate of performance on unseen firms rather than an artefact of the resampling.

The same loop also selects each model's hyperparameters through a nested grid search
inside the cross-validation. Every candidate configuration is put through the full
evaluation and scored by its fold-averaged $F_2$, which is how the $\ell_1$ penalty
of the logistic regression, the \texttt{max\_features} of the random forest and the
depth and learning rate of gradient boosting are chosen. The searched grids and the
complexity curves confirming that the selected configurations do not overfit are
collected in Appendix~\ref{ch:appendix}. Finally, the whole ten-fold split is repeated three times, each time with
a different randomization of the firms, which gives $30$ independent train/test
evaluations per model. The mean and standard deviation reported in
Chapter~\ref{ch:results} therefore no longer depend on any single split of the data.

\section{Performance Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}

All metrics derive from the confusion matrix, which cross-tabulates predicted
against actual classes into true positives (TP), false positives (FP), false
negatives (FN) and true negatives (TN), where the positive class is bankruptcy.
In standard statistical terminology a false positive is a Type I error and a false
negative a Type II error, which is also how \textcite[563]{liang2016} report their
results on this dataset. Table~\ref{tab:confusion-schema} fixes the notation.

\begin{table}[htbp]
  \centering
  \caption{Confusion-matrix notation, with bankruptcy as the positive class.}
  \label{tab:confusion-schema}
  \begin{tabular}{llcc}
    \toprule
    & & \multicolumn{2}{c}{\textbf{Predicted}} \\
    \cmidrule(lr){3-4}
    & & Bankrupt & Healthy \\
    \midrule
    \multirow{2}{*}{\textbf{Actual}}
      & Bankrupt & TP & FN \\
      & Healthy  & FP & TN \\
    \bottomrule
    \floatfoot{Source: own.}
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

From these counts, precision and recall are defined as
\begin{equation}
  \text{Precision} = \frac{\text{TP}}{\text{TP} + \text{FP}}, \qquad
  \text{Recall} = \frac{\text{TP}}{\text{TP} + \text{FN}},
  \label{eq:precrec}
\end{equation}
where precision is the share of firms flagged as bankrupt that actually went
bankrupt, and recall is the share of actually bankrupt firms that the model flagged. 
The two are combined by the $F_\beta$-score, a weighted harmonic mean,
\begin{equation}
  F_\beta = (1 + \beta^2)\,
  \frac{\text{Precision} \cdot \text{Recall}}
       {\beta^2 \cdot \text{Precision} + \text{Recall}},
  \label{eq:fbeta}
\end{equation}
which reduces to the familiar $F_1$ for $\beta = 1$. This thesis adopts $\beta = 2$
as the primary metric, which weights recall four times as heavily as precision. The
reason is the economic asymmetry of the problem. Failing to flag a firm that
subsequently goes bankrupt is far more costly than a false alarm that triggers
closer inspection of a healthy firm, a point that \textcite[117]{veganzones2018}
formalise through the expected cost of misclassification and that
\textcite[15]{ansahnarh2024} make directly when they note that missing a truly
bankrupt company calls for models with high recall. \textcite[8]{wangliu2021} set
$\beta = 2$ on this very dataset for the same reason, ``to minimize false
negatives'', which in addition preserves comparability with the benchmark this
thesis extends.

Accuracy and the area under the ROC curve are reported as secondary figures but were
never used for model selection. The accuracy trap was already visible in
Chapter~\ref{ch:dataset}, where a classifier labelling every firm healthy would
score above $96\%$ without ever identifying the cases of interest, and the ROC
curve, which weights both classes symmetrically, likewise shows an over-optimistic
picture under strong imbalance. The $F_2$-score is therefore the quantity that the
grid search of Section~\ref{sec:cv} maximises and the metric on which all decisions
are based.
