\chapter{Experimental Results}
\label{ch:results}

This chapter reports the results in two parts. Section~\ref{sec:res-performance}
presents the predictive performance of the three models, and
Section~\ref{sec:res-fi} presents the feature-importance rankings, whose
systematic divergence is the central empirical finding. All statements concern
predictive reliance only; the economic reading is deferred to
Chapter~\ref{ch:discussion}, and the associative caveat of
Chapter~\ref{ch:featureimportance} applies throughout.

Table~\ref{tab:performance} reports each model at its best hyperparameter
configuration, averaged over the $30$ cross-validation fits with standard
deviations. Read through the primary metric, the two tree ensembles clearly
outperform the linear baseline: gradient boosting attains the highest $F_2$ at
$0.550$, the random forest follows closely at $0.546$, and the logistic
regression trails at $0.482$. The pattern is consistent with the modelling
rationale of Section~\ref{sec:models}: the ensembles exploit threshold effects
and interactions that the linear model cannot represent. It is also instructive
that the ensembles achieve their advantage through recall --- both detect roughly
$74\%$ of bankruptcies against the logistic regression's $55\%$ --- which is
exactly the quantity the $F_2$-score is designed to reward. Precision is modest
for all three, and accuracy is omitted from selection for the reasons given in
Section~\ref{sec:metrics}; the high ROC-AUC values, all above $0.92$, illustrate
precisely how flattering that symmetric metric looks under imbalance.

The interpretive results are where the analysis becomes most informative. When
the three importance methods of Chapter~\ref{ch:featureimportance} are applied to
the three models, their top-ranked features diverge in a systematic way.
Table~\ref{tab:divergence} distils the pattern by reporting, for each model, the
single most important feature under each method.

\begin{table}[htbp]
  \centering
  \caption{Top-ranked feature under each importance method, by model. Values are
    the respective importance scores (signed coefficient for native LR;
    Gini importance for native RF/GB; mean $F_2$ drop for permutation;
    mean $|\text{SHAP}|$ for SHAP).}
  \label{tab:divergence}
  \begin{tabular}{llll}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Model} & \textbf{Native} & \textbf{Permutation} & \textbf{SHAP} \\
    \midrule
    Logistic Regression & Debt ratio \% ($+14.49$) & Debt ratio \% ($0.248$)
      & Debt ratio \% ($0.625$) \\
    Random Forest & Persistent EPS ($0.092$) & Interest Expense Ratio ($0.020$)
      & Persistent EPS ($0.026$) \\
    Gradient Boosting & Persistent EPS ($0.247$) & Borrowing dependency ($0.024$)
      & Borrowing dependency ($0.356$) \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

Three observations follow. First, for the logistic regression all three methods
agree: the \emph{Debt ratio} is unambiguously the dominant feature, and its
positive coefficient confirms the economically intuitive direction that higher
leverage raises the predicted probability of bankruptcy. The signed SHAP bar
chart for the logistic regression, Figure~\ref{fig:shap-lr}, reproduces this
ranking and its direction.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.82\textwidth]{shap_LogisticRegression_bar.png}
  \caption{Mean absolute SHAP values for the logistic regression (bankruptcy
    class). The \emph{Debt ratio} dominates, consistent with its native
    coefficient and permutation importance.}
  \label{fig:shap-lr}
\end{figure}

Second, for the tree ensembles the native Gini importance places \emph{Persistent
EPS in the Last Four Seasons} first in both models. This is exactly the pattern
that \textcite{strobl2007} would predict: a fine-grained continuous feature is
elevated by the impurity bias discussed in Section~\ref{sec:fi-native}. SHAP
partially corrects this. For gradient boosting in particular, SHAP demotes
Persistent EPS to second place and promotes \emph{Borrowing dependency} to first,
with a mean absolute value of $0.356$ against $0.292$ --- an economically more
plausible ordering, since borrowing dependency is a direct leverage measure. The
gradient-boosting SHAP summary in Figure~\ref{fig:shap-gb} displays this
corrected ranking together with the direction of each feature's effect.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.82\textwidth]{shap_GradientBoosting_beeswarm.png}
  \caption{SHAP summary (beeswarm) for gradient boosting, bankruptcy class.
    \emph{Borrowing dependency} ranks first, ahead of \emph{Persistent EPS},
    correcting the impurity-based ordering.}
  \label{fig:shap-gb}
\end{figure}

The correction is only partial for the random forest, whose SHAP ranking, shown
in Figure~\ref{fig:shap-rf}, still places Persistent EPS first with Borrowing
dependency close behind. That the same importance method yields a fully corrected
ordering for gradient boosting but only a partial one for the random forest
underlines that importance is a joint property of the method \emph{and} the model,
not of the data alone.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.82\textwidth]{shap_RandomForest_beeswarm.png}
  \caption{SHAP summary (beeswarm) for the random forest, bankruptcy class.
    \emph{Persistent EPS} remains first, with \emph{Borrowing dependency} second
    --- a weaker correction of the impurity ordering than for gradient boosting.}
  \label{fig:shap-rf}
\end{figure}

Third, permutation importance behaves very differently on the ensembles: its
values are an order of magnitude smaller and its ranking is unstable, with
minor features such as \emph{Interest Expense Ratio} surfacing at the top of the
random forest (Figure~\ref{fig:perm-rf}). This is the correlation-masking effect anticipated in
Section~\ref{sec:fi-permutation}: among $95$ collinear ratios, permuting any
single feature barely moves the score because a correlated substitute remains.
Figure~\ref{fig:perm-gb} shows the characteristically compressed permutation
profile for gradient boosting, where only \emph{Borrowing dependency} stands
clearly above the noise. The contrast between the informative SHAP ranking and
the flattened permutation ranking on the same model is itself a result, and it is
taken up in the discussion.

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.82\textwidth]{permutation_RandomForest.png}
  \caption{Permutation importance ($F_2$ drop) for the random forest. The top
    feature, \emph{Interest Expense Ratio}, differs from both the native and SHAP
    rankings, and all values are small --- the same correlation-masking signature
    seen for gradient boosting.}
  \label{fig:perm-rf}
\end{figure}
