\chapter{Dataset}
\label{ch:dataset}

The empirical analysis uses the Taiwanese Bankruptcy Dataset, a well-established
benchmark compiled from the Taiwan Economic Journal and covering listed firms
over the period 1999--2009. It is the same dataset studied by
\textcite{wangliu2021}, which makes the two works directly comparable. The data
comprise $6{,}819$ firm-year observations described by $95$ financial features
and a single binary target that records whether a firm was declared bankrupt.
Table~\ref{tab:dataset} summarises its composition.

\begin{table}[htbp]
  \centering
  \caption{Composition of the Taiwanese Bankruptcy Dataset.}
  \label{tab:dataset}
  \begin{tabular}{lrr}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Class} & \textbf{Firms} & \textbf{Share} \\
    \midrule
    Healthy (0)   & $6{,}599$ & $96.77\%$ \\
    Bankrupt (1)  & $220$     & $3.23\%$  \\
    \midrule
    Total         & $6{,}819$ & $100.00\%$ \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
  \floatfoot{Binary target; $95$ financial features per observation.}
\end{table}

The $95$ features span the standard categories of firm-level financial analysis:
profitability ratios such as return on assets, leverage and solvency ratios such
as the debt ratio, liquidity ratios, turnover and efficiency ratios, and
per-share value measures. The overwhelming majority are continuous ratios. Two
features are exceptions and are treated as categorical throughout: the
\emph{Liability-Assets Flag} and the \emph{Net Income Flag}, each taking the
values $0$ or $1$. Because they are indicators rather than magnitudes, they are
excluded from the continuous-feature normalisation described in
Section~\ref{sec:preprocessing}.

Two properties of the data shape the entire analysis. The first is the severe
class imbalance already visible in Table~\ref{tab:dataset}: with bankrupt firms
accounting for only $3.23\%$ of observations, a naive classifier can achieve high
accuracy while being predictively worthless, which motivates both the
undersampling design of Section~\ref{sec:undersampling} and the choice of the
$F_2$-score in Section~\ref{sec:metrics}. The second is the high dimensionality
combined with pronounced multicollinearity among the $95$ ratios: many features
are algebraic transformations or close relatives of one another, so that
distinct ratios carry overlapping information. This redundancy is largely benign
for predictive accuracy but has direct consequences for feature importance, as
Chapters~\ref{ch:featureimportance} and \ref{ch:results} will show. A number of
further questions --- the absence of macroeconomic controls, the sector
composition of the sample, and the precise legal definition of the bankruptcy
event --- are raised here but left open, since they bear on interpretation
rather than on the predictive exercise itself.
