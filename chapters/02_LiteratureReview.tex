\chapter{Literature Review}
\label{ch:literature}

The quantitative prediction of corporate bankruptcy has evolved through three
overlapping phases: classical statistical models, the adoption of machine
learning, and, most recently, a growing concern with class imbalance and model
interpretability. This chapter reviews the strands most relevant to the present
work and, in doing so, isolates the gap the thesis addresses. Following the
guidance of the chair, the discussion privileges peer-reviewed contributions in
established outlets and treats the more recent, method-heavy conference
literature with corresponding caution regarding the reliability of its
reported gains.

\section{From Statistical Models to Machine Learning}
\label{sec:lit-ml}

Early bankruptcy prediction relied on discriminant analysis and, later, on logit
and probit models estimated over a handful of financial ratios. These models are
transparent and their coefficients carry an economic sign, but they impose
strong functional-form assumptions and struggle with the non-linear
interactions that characterise firm-level data. The comprehensive study of
\textcite{liang2016} is representative of the transition to machine learning: it
combines financial ratios with corporate-governance indicators across a large
Taiwanese sample and shows that ensemble classifiers systematically outperform
single linear models. Their work also established much of the feature-category
groundwork on which subsequent studies, including this one, implicitly rely. At
the same time, \textcite{liang2016} adopt a forced balancing of the two classes,
a design choice that later work has questioned and that the present thesis
deliberately avoids.

\section{Class Imbalance and Sampling Strategies}
\label{sec:lit-imbalance}

Because bankruptcies are rare, the treatment of class imbalance has become a
defining methodological question. \textcite{veganzones2018} investigate the
point at which imbalance begins to degrade classifier performance and confirm
that standard learners deteriorate sharply as the minority share falls, making
some form of rebalancing necessary. Two broad families of remedies exist.
Oversampling methods, of which SMOTE is the archetype, synthesise additional
minority observations; undersampling methods instead reduce the majority class
until a workable ratio is reached. The most directly relevant contribution is
\textcite{wangliu2021}, who study undersampling on precisely the Taiwanese
dataset used here. Using a cluster-based undersampling technique together with
repeated stratified cross-validation and the $F_2$-score, they report a best
configuration of edited nearest neighbours combined with a naive-Bayes
classifier, reaching $F_2 = 0.423$. Their framework --- the sampling philosophy,
the leakage-aware validation, and the metric --- forms the methodological base
that this thesis extends.

A parallel and considerably larger body of recent work reports far higher
performance by combining synthetic oversampling with flexible learners.
\textcite{pham2025} evaluate multiple models on Taiwanese firms and reach
accuracies near $98.6\%$ using SMOTE together with an artificial neural network;
\textcite{suresh2025} and \textcite{sinap2025} report comparable figures with
related oversampling pipelines, the latter also employing SHAP for
interpretation. \textcite{liashenko2023} and \textcite{amirshahi2024} similarly
survey balancing methods and ensemble models under both balanced and imbalanced
regimes. These results are impressive at face value, but they share a common
vulnerability: a substantial fraction of the minority class on which the models
are trained --- and sometimes evaluated --- consists of firms that never
existed. Headline accuracy is, in addition, a misleading summary statistic under
a $96.8/3.2$ split. The present thesis treats this literature as a benchmark to
argue against rather than to emulate, prioritising the integrity of the training
data over marginal gains in reported performance.

\section{Feature Selection versus Feature Importance}
\label{sec:lit-fi}

A final strand concerns which features drive the predictions. Several studies
approach this through upfront feature \emph{selection}. \textcite{chaudhary2023},
for instance, uses Pearson correlation to screen features on the Taiwanese
market before fitting a model. Such filters are simple and intuitive, but they
capture only linear association and, more importantly, answer a different
question from the one posed here: they rank features \emph{before} and
independently of the fitted model, whereas this thesis asks which features the
\emph{trained} model actually relies upon. Post-hoc importance methods have
themselves been applied in the bankruptcy context ---
\textcite{ansahnarh2024} report random-forest importances alongside a
cluster-centroid undersampling step, and \textcite{ko2024} combine factor
analysis with several learners on the same Taiwanese data --- yet these studies
typically report a single importance measure without examining whether
alternative measures would agree. This is precisely the gap the thesis targets:
rather than adopting one importance method, it compares native importance,
permutation importance, and SHAP, and makes the disagreement between them the
object of study. The methodological foundations for that comparison ---
the impurity bias of tree-based importances documented by \textcite{strobl2007},
the tuning behaviour of random forests analysed by \textcite{probst2019}, and
the unified Shapley framework of \textcite{lundberg2017} --- are introduced where
they are used, in Chapters~\ref{ch:methodology} and \ref{ch:featureimportance}.
